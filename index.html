About Me

Before getting into computer science, I had a background in chemistry and biology, delighting in the way that our world can be broken down into processes and molecules that completely dominate our life. As I entered college, I became aware of another fundamental aspect of this world: networks. Our world is inevitably interconnected by the networks of computers and information that allows us to reach people from different sides of the earth. Computer Science completely enthralled me, and since then, I have continued exploring into the world of computers. Mirroring my love of computer science is my love for consulting, for working with people to create practical solutions to plaguing issues. I love the innovation that goes into consulting, and I want to apply that creativity to my everyday life. In consulting, I will go above and beyond for my clients in order to deliver the solution that they need.

MIT Media Lab

Undergraduate Researcher• February 2017 - June 2017

I realized that I was interested in machine learning, so during spring semester of 2017, I joined the Affective Computing group at the MIT Media Lab. This group worked at the intersection of human dynamics and machine interaction. I personally worked with the NAO robot by designing a complete cycle of motions to test human-robot interaction. I also worked with several machine-learning algorithms with tensorflow that involved facial detection. This expanded my passion for machine learning, and I have realized that I would love to further pursue projects in that field. Ultimately, my UROP supervisor presented our work at a Happiness conference in Miami which was an extremely gratifying experience.

AINS

Web Development Intern• January 2017 - Febuary 2017

I worked with AINS, a company that specializes in designing and implementing automatic workflow management. I worked with another intern to develop a web application that uses the principle behind Google's Blockly in order to create an application that allows users without programming experience to drag blocks of words into a formation that translates into code. This web application was to be integrated into their management workflow so that they could develop tickets for task completion without writing any code.

MIT D-Lab

Undergraduate Researcher• October 2016 - December 2016

In order to get more experience with electrical engineering, I participated in a research project in which I worked to develop a circuit that is able amplify the signal from a carbon monoxide detector. The overall purpose is to test if the adoption of a cookstove in third-world countries can decrease the air pollution from cooking. A secondary goal was to add a small indicator to give immediate responses regarding the air-quality in order to test if this can influence adoption patterns. Some issues that we faced in this project included creating a design that is both power-efficient and accurate. I presented my work in the semi-annual D-Lab showcase in front of dozens of students as well as genearal members of the MIT community.
